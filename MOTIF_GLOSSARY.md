## Chapter 5 — கருக்கணிகள் (*Karukkanigal* — Curvilinear Scroll Motifs)

### Scrolls — சுருள்கள் (*Surulgal*)

In the Maharatham (temple complex), the lower base (*peedam* / பீடம்), the leg section (*muzhangkaal paagam* / முழங்கால் பாகம்), the thigh section (*thodai paagam* / தொடை பாகம்), and the hip section (*iduppu paagam* / இடுப்பு பாகம*) — across all these parts of the temple body, numerous "scrolls" (*vaar* / வார்) are present.

In the first, third, and fourth tiers on all four sides combined, a total of 14 panels contain 56 deity figures.

In the *Poodapparil* (பூதப்பாரில்), on both front and back, 5 floral figures (*pootha uruvangal*) per side totalling 10 are arranged. On the second tier, 9 horse rows (*kuthirai varigal*) per side yield 36 horses (*puravigal*); 8 lion-figures (*simmangal*) per side give 32 lions on all four sides; 3 *yaali* figures per side across four sides equals 12; and between them, 2 lions with spread wings (*sirikkai virittha asuvangal*) per side across 4 sides totals 8.

### Foundation — ஆதாரம் (*Aadhaaram*)

The scrolls combined with creepers are called *'karukkugal'* (கருக்குகள்) — the sculptors (*silpi* / சில்பி) named them so. The *karukkanigal* are designed based on the foundational compositions (*aadhaaramaaga irukkak koodiya amaippugalai*).

With so many deity forms, various animal sculptures arranged with great finesse — one intertwined behind another — spread, curved, winding, and extending (*nelintha virintha*) — with buds, leaves, flowers (*arumbu, ilai, pushpangaludan*) — the creeper scrolls are composed in many forms (*designs*).

### The Nine Scrolls — நவ கருக்குகள் (*Nava Karukkugal*)

In many important places, the softness (*merugai*) and beauty (*azhagai*) of these scrolls are utilised. The sculptors have compiled them as the **Nine Scrolls** (*Nava Karukkugalaga*):

### 1. அன்னவால் கருக்கு — *Annavaal Karukku* (Swan-tail Scroll)

Goddess Saraswathi (*Kalaivaaniyanaa Sridevi*) has the swan (*annam*) as her vehicle (*vaaganam*). Using their imaginative power (*karpanaa sakthi*), sculptors have created the swan's tail as a *'Merukoottu'* (elaborate embellishment) motif.

This pattern is also used in: the tail of the *makaram* (mythical aquatic creature), the peacock's tail (*mayilin thogai*), the conch shell's outer rim (*sangin oliveli*), canopy edges (*kapothaak koodugal*), and on the lateral panels of some crowns (*kireedangalin pakkavaattil*).

### 2. குதிரைவால் கருக்கு — *Kuthiraivaal Karukku* (Horse-tail Scroll)

Its origin: the horse-tail's dense and wavy (*neelam — asaivu*) qualities closely match this scroll type. Hence it is named after the horse-tail.

It is also used in the *makaram*, peacock, and *thiruvaaṭchi* (royal fan / *pirapai*) motifs.

### 3. சுடர்க் கருக்கு — *Sudark Karukku* (Flame Scroll)

Shaped like a lamp-flame (*deepchudar*), these scrolls are especially used in *thiruvaaṭchi* (திருவாட்சி) — royal fans. They appear on either side of the *Mahanasi* (main dormer window / கோபுரங்கள்). In *gopuram* structures, this motif is arranged on both flanks.

### 4. கொடிக்கருக்கு — *Kodikkarukku* (Creeper Scroll)

This scroll is arranged on decorative bands (*pattikaikal*). Additionally, in temple pillars (*koavil thoonkal*), these scrolls are beautifully composed. At entrance thresholds (*vaayilnilaippadi*), especially in the main *Thorana Thiruvaaayil* (ornamental gateway arch), these scrolls are arranged. Buds, leaves, flowers — all winding, curving, and intertwined one behind another — produce the beauty.

In chariots (*rathangal*), on long and short decorative bands, these creeper scrolls occupy the primary position. A special quality: these scrolls have *'no boundary'* (*ellai illai*). To whatever height or length, they can curve, wind, shrink, and continue endlessly. Great *'imaginative power'* (*karpanaa sakthi*) is needed to create them. That alone suffices.

### 5. சமச்சீர் கோடிப்பாளைக் கருக்கு — *Samaccheer Kodippaalai Karukku* (Symmetrical Sheath Scroll)

Two levels (*mattangal*), two tips (*munaikal*), meeting at a corner point form a *'Kodi'* (கோடி — apex/edge). This forms a band shape (*pattikai*), descending symmetrically from both sides. Sculptors also call this *'Karna Paalikai'* (கர்ண பாலிகா — ear-ornament band).

### 6. மையப் பாளைக் கருக்கு — *Maiyap Paalai Karukku* (Central Sheath Scroll)

Wherever two tips of creepers meet, the decorative beauty (*azhukooddum vannam*) applied at that junction in a symmetrical manner is the *Maiyap Paalai Karukku*.

In *vimana sikarangal* (tower pinnacles), *karna kudam* (corner vessels), ornamental platforms (*alangkaara thattugal*) — at the centre and main bands — these scrolls are specially arranged. They add extra beauty (*azhagu*) to already beautiful sections.

### 7. ஓடு கருக்கு — *Odu Karukku* (Running Scroll)

Arranged on vertical bands (*sengkutthu pattikai*), on the *vimaanam* and similar structures. Shoots (*thalirgal*), flowers (*pookkal*), leaves (*ilaikal*), and similar elements — winding, curving, twisting (*nelindhum, valaindhum, soondum*) — are composed. But this differs from a full creeper scroll: it is a single-creeper scroll only (*otraik kodiyodu koodiya karukku*).

### 8. வட்டக் கருக்கு — *Vattak Karukku* (Circular Scroll)

From within a circle — buds (*arumbu*), leaves (*ilai*), and similar elements radiate outward in a curved, spreading pattern. This circular scroll is also composed within it. In chariot (*ratham*) designs, the main circular elements are arranged with this scroll. It also appears in the thighs (*thodaikal*) of animals like lions (*simmam*), horses (*puravi*), and *yaali* (யாளி — mythical lion-beast). It is also found in pillars (*thoonkal*) and *pothigaikal* (போதிகைகள் — bracket capitals).

### 9. அடைப்புக் கருக்கு — *Adaippuk Karukku* (Enclosing/Filler Scroll)

In chariots, there are no completely empty sections. Even a small area, when an empty space occurs, is filled using the *Adaippuk Karukku*. On the northern face beneath the crown (*keenaasi*), and at the base of pillars (*thooni adip pakuthi*), wherever empty space appears — this filler scroll beautifies it. It is especially used to adorn vehicle-pedestals (*vaagana peedangal*).

### Lotus Garlands — செந்தாமரை மலர் (*Senthamarai Malar* — Red Lotus Flower)

Within the scrolls, certain flowers are depicted. The primary one is the lotus (*thaamarai malar*).

The poet Kambar praised it as *"Chittrathil ezhuthiya senthamarai"* ("A red lotus painted in a picture"). The fame of the lotus flower is such that no poet has failed to sing of it. No sculptor in the sculpture tradition has failed to carve the lotus. It holds a position no other can replace (*oru neekamara nilaiyai*). The large *Neelodhpala* lotus is primarily used.

The importance given to birds, animals, and flowers in sculpture is equally significant — they all receive the same careful treatment.

The lotus garland is divided into three types:

| # | Tamil | Transliteration | Description |
|---|-------|----------------|-------------|
| 1 | சத்திப்பூமாலை | *Satthip Poo Maalai* | Flowers arranged one above another in rows to resemble a garland, carved as a *thirattai* (twisted/layered) garland |
| 2 | திரிசூல மாலை | *Thirissoola Maalai* | Flowers strung in the shape of a trident (*thirisoolam*). Used for decorating *vimana sikaram* (tower pinnacles) |
| 3 | குழறு மாலை | *Kuzharu Maalai* | A noted garland using various leaves and flowers, also called *'Kadhampa'* maalai. Used in *gopura sikarangal* (gateway tower pinnacles). Honey-gathering is an essential thematic element to be integrated where tree-related sections of the temple require it. |

### Banana Flower — கதலிப்பூ (*Kadhalippoo*)

The next remarkable flower is the *'Vaazhippu'* (வாழைப்பூ — banana flower/blossom). In tree-form decorative pillars (*marathil alangkaara thoonkal*), small vessels (*siriya kalasangal*) are arranged and the banana blossom motif is placed in that form at the *Pothigai* (bracket capital).

Additionally, the *Karṇakudam* (கர்ணகுடம் — corner vessel) atop the chariot must be topped with a *kalasam* (ஸ்தூபி — finial pot). The sequence within it follows: *Mahapadmam – Kandam – Aradha-Kandam – Kudam – Kandam – Aradha – Kandam – Alar* — and above all this comes the *'Kadhalippoo'* (banana blossom finial).

## Chapter 6 — அணிகலன்கள் (*Anikalankal* — Ornaments / Adornments)

### Elevated Recessed Panel — மேடு பள்ளம் (*Medu Pallam*)

*'Punnagaiye'* (புன்னகையே — "The Smile Itself") — described as a priceless gem (*'Ponnagai'* / பொன்னகை). From the very beginning, in every temple (*Thiru Thergal*), every deity sculpture and bas-relief has been carved with astonishing artistry through the sculptor's smile-like touch (*punnagaiyaal*). How, then, are the many ornaments (*anikalankal*) arranged upon these sculptures?

Pillars (*kangal*), philosophical concepts (*karuthukkal*), and the adornments on sculptures — all serve the same overarching purpose. In the body, the raised and recessed surfaces (*medu pallam*) must be arranged without any diminishment in visual quality. The ornaments must be composed with care — not a single element should fall below standard.

The Sthapathi (master architect-sculptor) Doctor of Philosophy *Kanapathi Sthapathiyar* states regarding this craft:

> *"… In coloured paintings and in stone sculptures alike, when the colour scheme is well-planned, the ornaments stand out distinctly, clearly, and with vivid definition…"*
